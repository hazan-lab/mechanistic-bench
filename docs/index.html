Summary

Comparing sequence-mixer architectures at the scale where their behavior matters (a billion parameters or more) costs multiple GPU-days per run, which is out of reach for most academic labs. We ask a simpler question first: if we train the same architectures as tiny 1M-parameter models on a battery of synthetic capability probes — induction, associative recall, copy, finite-state tracking, parity, and others — does their average score tell us which architecture will reach the lowest training loss once scaled up? It does. The cheap 1M-scale ranking matches the expensive downstream ranking closely enough to use as a screen, turning a decision that used to cost GPU-days into one that costs GPU-minutes.

Abstract

We propose a battery of mechanistic capability probes as a cheap behavioral screen for dense sequence-mixer architectures, and ask whether aggregate suite accuracy predicts downstream language-model training cross-entropy. On a held-out set of four architectures at 150M parameters we find Spearman ρ = −0.80 and Pearson r = −0.97; the screen is robust to dropping any single task family, to the choice of aggregation rule, and — in a five-seed replication — to seed variation; the small-scale ranking direction is preserved at 1B on the two architectures we ran. Per-task profiles motivate Hydra, a multi-head block that places attention, STU, and Mamba mixers as parallel heads within each layer; Hydra matches or beats a parameter-matched 1B OLMo-2 attention baseline on training cross-entropy and on a majority of zero-shot benchmarks.

The screen predicts downstream loss

We train each architecture as a 1M-parameter model on every probe, take the mean accuracy as a single score, and correlate it against the downstream training cross-entropy of the same architecture scaled up. The ranking transfers: the small-scale score orders architectures the way the expensive run does.

Every mixer has a bottleneck

Breaking the suite down by primitive family shows where each architecture fails. Pure-spectral mixers (STU) collapse on retrieval while staying competitive on aggregation; attention is the opposite balance. No single mixer is best everywhere, which is what motivates a hybrid.

Hydra

Because the probe profile shows attention, Mamba, and STU each own a different primitive, Hydra places all three as parallel heads inside every block, so each layer can route capacity to the computation that suits it. At 1B parameters, trained on OLMo-Mix-1124, Hydra reaches a lower training cross-entropy than a parameter-matched OLMo-2 attention baseline (2.880 versus 2.934) and wins a majority of zero-shot benchmarks.

Robustness

  • Seed stability: a five-seed replication of the 1M sweep moves suite means by at most 1.1 points and preserves the ranking on four of five seeds.
  • Dropping any single primitive family keeps the correlation negative and significant.
  • Median and trimmed-mean scoring reproduce the correlations; only the single-hardest-task minimum degrades them.
  • The retrieval gap between attention and non-attention mixers holds at every learning rate we tried, so it is architectural rather than an optimizer artifact.
  • Adding a Mamba-2 and an out-of-lineage LSTM keeps the aggregate correlation strong (up to ρ=−0.86), though we do not yet trust the exact placement of architectures outside the family the suite was developed on.
